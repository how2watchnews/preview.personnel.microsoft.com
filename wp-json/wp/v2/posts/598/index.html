If a steaming bowl of soup strikes you as the ultimate in old-fashioned comfort, you\u2019ve got plenty of company. Soup is one of the world\u2019s oldest and most universal foods, said Janet Clarkson, author of the book Soup: A Global History. Every culture has some kind of soup, she said. It\u2019s got very ancient roots. Early people simmered it in everything from turtle shells to lengths of bamboo, she writes in the book, turning out metal soup pots starting in the Bronze Age.<\/p>\n

Boiling food made it possible to subsist on stable grains, with herbs and other ingredients added for nourishment or medicinal purposes. Each time you deliver a pot of hearty soup (with perhaps a side of bread) to a friend with the sniffles, Clarkson said you\u2019re in fact carrying on an age-old tradition. Separating food and medicine \u2014 that\u2019s not how ancient people thought of it, she said. I think in every country in the world, historically, some soups were seen as restorative.<\/p>\n

That\u2019s true no matter what you call it. Today, soup leans brothy while stews are more substantial, but the world\u2019s spoonable foods have never fit neatly into the two English-language categories. While Clarkson dove into centuries of etymology to trace the history of soup, potage and broth, she settled on a generously broad take. Just some stuff cooked in water, she wrote, with the flavored water becoming a crucial part of the dish. It\u2019s a definition that leaves room for the world\u2019s tremendous culinary diversity. These are CNN Travel\u2019s nominations for 20 of the best soups around the world:<\/p>\n

Banga | Nigeria<\/h4>\n

Fruits from the oil palm tree lend both fat and flavor to this soup from the Niger Delta, which also features fresh catfish, beef and dried seafood. It\u2019s so popular that packets of ready-mixed banga spice are sold in shops. Most blends include African nutmeg, castor seed, orima, jansa and beletete leaves. The spices infuse a rich, red sauce that\u2019s the soup\u2019s main draw: Soak it up with eba or a ball of starch, two Nigerian staples both made from cassava prepared with different methods.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
